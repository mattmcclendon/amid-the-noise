## The situation

Autodesk did not have a single system for licensing.

It had many.

Different products. Different teams. Different models of ownership. Each system carried part of the truth. None carried all of it. Customers experienced this as friction. Internally, it showed up as delays, rework, and uncertainty around who could actually make a decision.

At the same time, the company was moving toward cloud-based licensing and subscription models. The direction was clear. The system was not.

---
## Where the work actually started

I did not start with design.

I started with ownership.

Who owns each system.  
Who can change it.  
Who blocks it.  
Where decisions are made versus where they are recorded.

I audited the existing landscape, not just technically but organizationally. The map that emerged was not a clean architecture diagram. It was a network of influence.

That network determined what was possible.

---
## Operating without authority

I did not have formal authority over most of the systems involved.

Progress depended on alignment.

That meant building relationships across product, engineering, support, and operations. It meant making the system legible to the people inside it. Not through abstraction, but through shared visibility into how work actually moved.

Transparency became the first tool.

When people could see the full path of a licensing decision, including where it stalled or fractured, alignment stopped being theoretical. It became practical.

---
## Converging the system

The goal was not to replace everything.

It was to create a point of convergence.

I led the design and development of a back office portal that became the convergence layer for licensing. It unified how Autodesk customers managed licenses, credits, and subscriptions. The portal did not erase the underlying systems. It coordinated them.

For the first time, there was a place where:

- licensing could be managed, not just recorded  
- subscription models could operate alongside legacy structures  
- customers and internal teams could act from a shared view of the system  

The interface was visible. The real work was in how decisions flowed beneath it.

---
## What changed

Licensing moved from fragmented ownership to coordinated behavior.

Teams that previously operated in isolation began working from shared context. Support friction dropped because fewer issues required translation across systems. Decisions that once stalled could now resolve within a single flow.

Efficiency improved, but not because of optimization in isolation.

It improved because the system became coherent.

---
## The signal the system sent back

The work did not stay local.

As the system stabilized, the effects became visible beyond the teams directly involved. Licensing moved faster. Ownership became clearer. The organization began to trust the system to carry real work.

I was later named to Autodesk’s CEO Top 5 Up-and-Coming list.

The recognition mattered less than what it reflected: the system had started to hold.

---
## Then and now

This work took place in 2012.

The tools were different. The constraints were not.

Systems were fragmented. Ownership was unclear. Decisions happened across boundaries without a shared model.

That pattern has not changed. It has expanded.

I’ve seen this pattern hold across finance, automation, civic systems, and national security contexts.

The contexts change. The structure does not.

Make the system legible.  
Align the people inside it.  
Create a place where decisions can resolve.

---
## What this proves

This was not a design problem in the narrow sense.

It was a system problem shaped by:

- fragmented ownership  
- competing incentives  
- legacy constraints  
- organizational inertia  

The solution required more than interface design. It required aligning people, systems, and decisions under real constraints.

---
## Why this matters now

Most AI systems face the same underlying challenge.

Multiple sources of truth.  
Unclear ownership.  
Decisions happening in fragments across systems.

The interface is not the hard part.

Alignment is.

The work at Autodesk was an early version of this problem. The tools have changed. The structure has not.

---
## The throughline

I design systems where decisions have to hold under real-world conditions.

Not in theory.  
In organizations.  
With constraints.
