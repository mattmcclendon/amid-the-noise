The strange thing about the La Brea Tar Pits is that the ground never fully agrees to become ground.

The earth bubbles there.

Not metaphorically.
Literally.

Tar pushes upward through cracks in the landscape while office towers, museums, traffic, and morning joggers continue around it as though this is perfectly normal behavior for a city.

In some ways, perhaps it is.

Los Angeles has always felt slightly geologically unresolved:
fault lines,
hillsides,
canyons,
oil fields,
flood channels,
wildfire corridors,
aqueduct dependence.

The city often feels less constructed than negotiated.

The tar pits simply make that truth visible.

I remember the bubbles most clearly.

Not dramatic eruptions.
Just slow movement rising through the surface beside chain-link fences and museum pathways. A reminder that the earth beneath the city is still active, still shifting, still speaking in its own language regardless of zoning maps or development plans.

There was something deeply beautiful about the juxtaposition:
streetlights glowing in early morning haze,
the skyline in the distance,
prehistoric death rising quietly through the ground.

Thousands of animals vanished there because the surface appeared stable until it wasn’t.

That detail lingers with me.

Not as apocalypse.
Not as warning.

More as perspective.

Cities often present themselves as permanent. The La Brea Tar Pits undermine that illusion in the gentlest possible way. They remind visitors that history is not fully buried beneath civilization. Sometimes it rises directly through it.

Last time I visited, the newer museum did not yet exist. I mostly remember the older structures, rows of lights, and the strange stillness that hangs over the park in the early morning before traffic fully takes hold of the city.

I have since read that the area has changed dramatically over the past several years.

That feels appropriate somehow.

Los Angeles constantly reinvents the structures around the tar pits while the earth beneath them continues behaving exactly as it always has.

The city evolves.
The ground remembers.
