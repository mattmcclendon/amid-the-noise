Watching *Toy Story* with Gino today, I found myself laughing at a line I remembered very differently as a child.

> "You are a sad, strange, little man, and you have my pity."

When I was young, I thought Buzz was being a jerk.

Today, the line lands differently.

Woody spends the first part of the film unraveling. He mocks Buzz. He undermines him. He throws the Magic 8-Ball behind the dresser. Eventually, Buzz ends up out the window.

As a kid, I barely noticed any of that.

I noticed Woody.

I noticed Buzz.

I noticed Rex.

I definitely noticed Pizza Planet.

Gino first watched *Toy Story* when he was about three years old. Back then, the movie was not really a story in the way adults think about stories. It was a collection of things he loved.

**Woody!**

**Buzz!**

**Rex!**

**Pizza Planet!**

The names themselves were exciting. The characters were the experience.

Watching it again now, at six years old, I can see the story beginning to take shape.

He notices that Woody gets jealous. He notices that Buzz gets pushed out the window. He notices that Sid is scary. He notices that the toys have to find their way home.

He is assembling events now. The plot is becoming visible.

What fascinates me is that he still is not watching the same movie I am.

When I watch *Toy Story* now, I spend less time thinking about Buzz's gadgets and more time thinking about Woody's motivations.

I think about insecurity. About status. About what happens when someone fears being replaced.

Looking back, Buzz was not really insulting Woody.

He was identifying something Woody could not yet see in himself.

The movie itself has not changed.

Gino has.

So have I.

For a while, I thought children simply understood stories with less vocabulary than adults. Now I am not convinced that is what happens at all.

I think they experience a different story.

A three-year-old sees characters.

A six-year-old sees events.

An adult sees motivations.

None of those interpretations are wrong. Each reflects a different stage of understanding.

What struck me is that we never really stop doing this.

We become more sophisticated, but we still gravitate toward different layers of the same story.

Some people focus on the characters.

Some focus on the events.

Some look for the motivations underneath.

The same pattern shows up everywhere.

Two people can witness the same argument. One remembers what was said. The other remembers why it was said.

Two people can look at a civic failure. One sees the politician. Another sees the crisis. A third sees the incentives that made the outcome predictable long before anyone noticed.

The event is easy to spot.

The context takes longer.

Maybe that is why nuance is so difficult. It requires us to hold multiple layers of the same story in our heads at once.

The line only works because Woody eventually changes.

That is the part I missed as a child.

The older I get, the more I think that is true of most stories.

We notice the characters first.

Then the events.

The motivations often take years.

The movie never changed.

The viewer did.
