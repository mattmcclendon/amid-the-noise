AI isn’t a single breakthrough. It’s a stack.

Most people encounter the top layer and assume that’s the system. In reality, it’s just the interface. What sits underneath is decades of iteration, each layer compensating for the limits of the one before it.

Credit to Luís Rodrigues for the visual inspiration.

Here’s the stack, bottom to top:

## 1. Classical AI  
We began with certainty: rules, logic, explicit instruction. Systems worked as long as the world behaved predictably, which it rarely does.

## 2. Machine Learning  
We shifted from telling systems what to do to letting them infer patterns from data. Prediction replaced instruction. Accuracy improved, but understanding remained implicit and often opaque.

## 3. Neural Networks  
To handle complexity, we borrowed from biology. Layered architectures made messy inputs tractable, but the tradeoff was clarity. As capability increased, interpretability began to erode.

## 4. Deep Learning  
Scale accelerated everything. With more data and more parameters, systems moved beyond pattern detection into pattern modeling. Performance climbed, while control became increasingly statistical.

## 5. Generative AI  
Systems began producing language, images, and code—outputs that resemble intent closely enough to feel like understanding. This is where most conversations about AI begin, even though it sits near the top of the stack.

## 6. Agentic AI  
Now systems act. They retain memory, plan, use tools, and execute tasks across steps. At this layer, the shift is no longer about output quality but about delegated behavior.

---

Understanding the stack changes the decision you’re actually making.

You’re not evaluating a tool in isolation. You’re deciding where a system is allowed to act without direct oversight, and how much uncertainty you’re willing to absorb as a result.

Most conversations stay focused on what the system says.

The real question is what it’s allowed to do.
