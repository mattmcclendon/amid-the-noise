Every train that arrives and departs carries someone on a journey to a better part of themselves. Maybe they are running an errand, grabbing dinner, or rushing home to water the plants. It appears ordinary, almost mechanical. Yet in those movements, something sacred happens: the quiet rhythm of becoming.

For years, I fought this city—its chaos, contradictions, and sharp edges. I blamed it for what I couldn’t name in myself. I used to think peace came from control: getting ahead, looking polished, carrying the right bag, living the right life. I know now that was just noise.

Somewhere between the stations, I stopped fighting the current and began moving with it. The energy that once drained me now propels me forward. I notice things again: the light on stained glass, the fake sunflowers on a bar door, the laughter on the platform. San Francisco hasn’t changed. I have.

I’ve experienced seasons of wealth—the kind that shines under boutique lighting. I expect I’ll experience that again, probably. The difference now is that I no longer need it to feel real. I buy what I love, use what I buy, and let life be what it is.

This is not a recovery story. It is a story of being. Of presence. Of realizing that every city, like every person, contains both ruin and renewal at the same time, and that the freedom to stop fighting is the start of both.

The air is cool tonight in San Francisco. Crisp, imperfect, carrying the scent of the city—part asphalt, part autumn, part life. I tilt my face into it and breathe. It has been years since I truly felt a moment like this. The last time might have been on the playground at Sunset Elementary, third grade, when the world still felt new and simple.

Now, decades later, I find myself here again—not the same, but not entirely different either. Just a man letting the wind remind him that he is still alive, still learning, still becoming.
