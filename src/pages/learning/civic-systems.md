I worked inside an election system.

Not as an observer. As part of the operation.

The assumption going in was straightforward: if procedures were clear and followed precisely, the system would hold.

That’s not what I saw.

---

Procedures matter.

They create consistency. They define boundaries. They give the system shape.

But procedures don’t make decisions.

People do.

---

The system was designed to handle volume, variation, and scrutiny.

Voters showing up in the wrong county.

Names that didn’t match exactly.

Ballots that couldn’t be verified immediately.

Situations that didn’t fit cleanly into predefined paths.

In those moments, the system didn’t run itself.

It relied on interpretation.

---

Two people could follow the same procedure and arrive at different outcomes.

Not because one was wrong, but because the procedure required judgment.

When to escalate.

When to provisionally accept.

When to slow down.

When to let someone through.

---

That’s where trust lives.

Not in the rulebook.

In how consistently people interpret it under pressure.

---

The environment makes that pressure visible.

Lines forming.

Voters frustrated or anxious.

Time constraints tightening as the day moves on.

Every decision becomes public, observable, and consequential.

There is no abstraction layer.

---

What I learned is simple.

A system can be procedurally correct and still fail in practice.

Because the real system isn’t just the rules.

It’s the interaction between rules, people, and edge cases.

---

This changed how I think about design.

I don’t just ask whether a system is defined.

I ask how it behaves when definition runs out.

Where interpretation begins.

And whether the people inside it are supported well enough to make decisions that preserve trust.

---

That’s the work.

Designing systems that don’t just operate correctly, but hold under pressure when people have to decide what correctness means.
